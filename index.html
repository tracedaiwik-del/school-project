Understanding the Crisis

What is Plastic Pollution?

A Growing Threat to Our Planet

Plastic pollution occurs when plastic objects and particles accumulate in the environment, harming wildlife, wildlife habitats, and humans. Plastics are inexpensive and durable, making them popular — but these same qualities make them a serious environmental problem.

Unlike organic materials, plastic does not biodegrade. Instead, it photodegrades, breaking down into smaller and smaller pieces called microplastics that persist in the environment for hundreds — sometimes thousands — of years.

From remote mountain peaks to the deepest ocean trenches, plastic has been found everywhere on Earth. It contaminates our food chain, poisons our oceans, and endangers the survival of countless species — including our own.

The Impact

Effects of Plastic Pollution

Click each section to learn more about the devastating consequences

Ocean plastic is devastating marine ecosystems worldwide. Sea turtles mistake plastic bags for jellyfish, their primary food source. Seabirds feed plastic pieces to their chicks, causing starvation. Whales have been found dead with stomachs full of plastic debris.


Microplastics — tiny plastic fragments — are now found in the stomachs of fish, shellfish, and nearly all marine species studied. These particles carry toxic chemicals that accumulate in the food chain, becoming more concentrated in predators at the top — including humans who eat seafood.


Key impacts: Entanglement in plastic nets and rings, starvation from eating plastic, habitat destruction from plastic smothering coral reefs, and toxic contamination of the entire marine food chain.

Research shows that humans consume an average of 5 grams of microplastics every week — roughly the weight of a credit card. These particles enter our bodies through the food we eat, the water we drink, and even the air we breathe.


Plastic contains and absorbs harmful chemicals including BPA (bisphenol A), phthalates, and dioxins. These are known endocrine disruptors that can interfere with hormones, cause developmental problems in children, and increase cancer risk.


Key impacts: Hormone disruption, increased cancer risk, developmental problems in children, respiratory issues from microplastic inhalation, and contamination of drinking water sources globally.

Plastic and climate change are deeply connected. The production of plastic relies heavily on fossil fuels — oil and natural gas. Manufacturing plastic releases significant amounts of greenhouse gases, contributing to global warming at every stage of its life cycle.


When plastic is incinerated (burned) to dispose of it, it releases toxic gases and carbon dioxide. When it breaks down in sunlight, it emits methane and ethylene — powerful greenhouse gases. Even microplastics floating in the ocean affect the ability of plankton to absorb CO₂.


Key impacts: Plastic production emits 850 million metric tons of greenhouse gases annually. By 2050, plastic could be responsible for 13% of the total global carbon budget if production continues at the current rate.
